Chapter 01

The middleman
model, explained.

You find someone who has something to sell. You find someone who wants to buy it. You charge more than the supplier charges you. The difference is yours.

That's it. That's the whole model. Everything else in this guide is just the mechanics of making that happen consistently and at scale.

What makes this work is something most people overlook: buyers and suppliers rarely find each other efficiently on their own. Suppliers are great at delivering — not always at selling. Buyers are clear on what they need — not always on where to find it. You solve both problems simultaneously, and you get paid for doing it.

@cashflowdylan Chapter 3 — Finding Suppliers
Chapter 03

Finding suppliers
who want brokers.

Not every supplier will work with a broker. The ones who will are the ones who are good at delivering but struggle with sales and lead generation. That's most of them.

The conversation with a supplier is straightforward: you tell them you have access to buyers and you'd like to offer their service at a margin. You ask for their best price, agree that the buyer never needs to know the split, and move forward when a deal comes together. Most suppliers — particularly in services and construction — are happy to work this way because it means new business they didn't have to find themselves.

Where to find suppliers

  • 1
    Trade directories and industry portalsMost industries have directories of registered suppliers. Construction, cleaning, logistics, staffing — they all have platforms where businesses list themselves. These are warm leads: they want work.
  • 2
    LinkedIn and direct outreachSearch for businesses in your target industry, identify decision-makers, and reach out directly. A simple message explaining you broker contracts and are looking for reliable supply partners gets responses more often than you'd expect.
  • 3
    Word of mouth and referralsOnce you've done one deal, your supplier network grows. Every supplier knows other suppliers. Referrals are the fastest way to build a reliable supply side once you're moving.
  • 4
    Industries you already knowThe easiest place to start is an industry where you already have contacts or knowledge. You understand the pricing, you know the players, and you can spot a good deal faster than an outsider.
What to ask a supplier

"I work with buyers who need [service/product]. What's your best price if I bring you a confirmed deal? I handle the client relationship — I just need your number so I know what I'm working with."

What makes a good supplier relationship

  • They deliver consistently — your reputation is on the line, not just theirs
  • They're open to broker arrangements and won't cut you out of the deal
  • Their pricing leaves enough room for your margin and still competitive for the buyer
  • They're responsive — slow suppliers kill deals
  • They have capacity — no point selling work a supplier can't fulfil

Chapter 04

Finding buyers
who are ready to go.

A buyer is anyone with a need and a budget. Your job is to find them before they find a supplier on their own.

The buyer side of the equation is where most new brokers overthink things. You don't need a massive audience or an established brand. You need to be in front of people who have a problem your supplier solves — and that's a targeting exercise, not a popularity contest.

Where buyers are already looking

Government Tender Portals

Government agencies at federal, state, and local level publish contracts they need filled. AusTender, BuyNSW, VendorPanel — these are live buyer needs with budgets attached.

Business Networks & LinkedIn

Businesses regularly post about operational needs. Industry groups, LinkedIn posts, and direct outreach to operations managers can surface buyers faster than most people expect.

Your Existing Network

Most people know at least one business owner or decision-maker who needs something sourced. Your first deal is almost always closer than you think.

Industry-Specific Platforms

Real estate portals, construction tender boards, freight exchanges, staffing platforms — every industry has a marketplace. Learn where buyers congregate in your target sector.

"Your first buyer is probably someone you already know. Your second comes from the first deal going well."

How to approach a buyer

Keep it simple. You're not pitching a business — you're solving a problem they already have. Lead with the outcome, not the process.

  • Identify their need before you reach out — don't pitch blind
  • Lead with what you can deliver and at what price — not how you source it
  • The buyer doesn't need to know your supplier arrangement — only that you can deliver
  • Get a clear scope before going back to your supplier for a final quote
  • Move quickly — buyers who are actively looking won't wait long

Chapter 05

Structuring the deal
so you always get paid.

The model only works long-term if you're protected. That means getting the basics of deal structure right from the start.

  • 1
    Get supplier pricing in writingAlways confirm the supply price in writing before quoting a buyer. Verbal agreements change. If your supplier's price shifts after you've quoted the buyer, your margin disappears.
  • 2
    Use a simple brokering agreementA one-page agreement between you and your supplier confirming they won't contact your buyer directly is standard practice. It protects your position in the deal and is widely accepted across industries.
  • 3
    Invoice the buyer, pay the supplierYou invoice the buyer at your price. Once payment clears, you pay the supplier their price. The difference is yours. Keep a clean paper trail for every transaction.
  • 4
    Clarify scope before committingThe most common reason deals fall apart is scope creep — the buyer expects more than was agreed. Define deliverables clearly before the contract is signed.
Chapter 06

Deal cycles — small
vs high ticket.

One of the questions I get most is whether to start with small deals or go straight for high ticket. My answer: start where your confidence is, but understand the trade-offs.

Small Deals (days–2 weeks)

Lower margin per deal but fast cycle times. Great for building confidence, refining your process, and generating cash flow quickly. Services, supplies, and small contracts typically sit here.

High Ticket (weeks–months)

Larger margin but longer sales cycle and more complexity. One deal can generate more than months of small deals combined. Requires more relationship-building and negotiation skill.

The smart approach

Do small deals to learn the model and build cash flow. Use the confidence and process you develop to pursue high ticket deals. Most successful brokers run both simultaneously — a pipeline of small deals that pays the bills while one or two large deals move slowly in the background.

Chapter 07

What to do in
week one.

Most people read guides like this and do nothing. The ones who actually make money are the ones who take one concrete step before they feel ready. Here's exactly what that looks like.

  • 1
    Pick one industry to start inDon't try to broker across five industries at once. Pick one — ideally one you have some existing knowledge or contacts in — and go deep on it first. You can expand later.
  • 2
    Find three potential suppliersIdentify three businesses in your chosen industry that could be supply partners. Research their services, check their reputation, and reach out to each with a simple introductory message about brokering arrangements.
  • 3
    Get at least one supply priceFrom the responses you get, secure at least one confirmed supply price in writing. This is your cost base. Everything you sell above this number is your margin.
  • 4
    Find one active buyer needCheck a government tender portal, post in a relevant LinkedIn group, or reach out to one person in your network who might need what your supplier offers. You're not looking for a perfect match — you're looking for a real need.
  • 5
    Make one introductionConnect the need to your supplier. Quote the buyer at your margin. If it closes, you've done your first deal. If it doesn't, you've learned more than any guide could teach you — and you do it again.
The most important thing

Your first deal will not be perfect. You'll probably underprice your margin, overcomplicate the conversation, or lose a deal you should have won. Do it anyway. Every broker I know made those same mistakes on their first deal — and closed their second deal significantly better.
